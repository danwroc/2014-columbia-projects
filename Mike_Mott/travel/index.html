Climb and Write: The Top Mountains of Literature and Adventure

“Mountain worship in one form or another is as old and as widespread as mankind.” - Douglas Freshfield.

There are plenty of things to climb, from our backyard trees to the neighbor's car. But mountains have called to writers for years past, leading to adventure you too can experience.

Read on. The mountains await.

John Muir: Yosemite Valley, California

After taking steamship passage from New York via Panama, John Muir arrived in San Francisco, California in 1868. Upon arrival, Muir immediately traveled to Yosemite for a week, a place he'd only read about.

He noted, at first glance, that, "he was overwhelmed by the landscape, scrambling down steep cliff faces to get a closer look at the waterfalls, whooping and howling at the vistas, jumping tirelessly from flower to flower."

Muir later served as a shepherd in Yosemite valley, climbed a number of the mountains there and hiked down old Indian trails. He designed mills, built cabins and most of all, wrote letters and stories in his journal.

Ernest Hemingway: Mount Kilimanjaro, Tanzania

Ernest Hemingway wrote one of his most well-known short stories about this mountain — fittingly, "The Snows of Kilimanjaro".

The story reflects concerns he held as both a writer and a person — as someone acquainted with the wealthy, he was concerned that aspect of his life would damage his integrity as an author.

Hemingway complained on his second trip to Mt. Kilimanjaro that it didn't show itself for three weeks, referencing the mountain's famous cloud cover.

James Hilton: "Shangri-La" and The Himalayas

James Hilton wrote Lost Horizon in 1938, a novel about the (supposed) fictional valley taken care of by lamas. The valley is described as mystical and harmonious, and since the story was published, "Shangri-La" has become a synonym for any place with those qualities.

There is speculation that Shangri-La is in the western end of the Himalayas, in the Hunza Valley in Northern Pakistan.

The Hunza valley, however, lacks Tibetan culture and Buddhism, a key element of Lost Horizon.

The Snow Leopard, by Peter Mattheiessen, deserves equal recognition when mentioning the Himalayas — it was his book, based on his expedition tracking the rare snow leopard, that The Economist said first truly "crystallised" the sense that one might find oneself in the mountains.

T.E. Lawrence: The Wadi Rum Monument

T.E. Lawrence, played by Peter O'Toole in Lawrence of Arabia traveled through the Wadi Rum Monument several times in 1917-18.

The Bedouin have climbed the sandstone mountains of Wadi Rum frequently, and so do visitors in the area.

One of the rock formations was even named, in the 1980s, "The Seven Pillars of Wisdom" after Lawrence's book written after the Arab Revolt.

Maurice Herzog: Annapurna, Nepal

Maurice Herzog was the first to climb Annapurna, the largest peak attained for three years until Everest's summit was reached.

His accomplishment sparked the writing of the the most highly-selling mountain novel ever afterwards: Annapurna. The book has sold over 11 million copies as of 2000.

Herzog went on to become a French minister and mayor, among other things.

Jon Krakauer: Mount Everest, Nepal/Tibet/China

Jon Krakauer's Into Thin Air tells the story of the 1996 Mount Everest disaster, which the author was present in. After a freak storm hit during a critical point in the climbing season, eight climbers were killed and several were stranded.

There has been not a little criticism of Krakauer's account of one of the climbers who rescued other climbers, claiming that Krakauer called his rescues a "fluke".

For the mountain itself, Mount Everest is the largest mountain on Earth, at just under 30,000 feet.

Joe Simpson: The Andes, Peru

Joe Simpson's Touching the Void, written in 1988, told the story of his fall into a crevasse three years earlier in the Peruvian Andes. The fall caused his leg to drive through his knee joint.

Miraculously, he survived, after draggin himself six miles through the Andes.

Needless to say, the book became very popular, selling hundreds of thousands of copies.

Meriwether Lewis and William Clark: The Rocky Mountains, USA

Lewis and Clark's Journals, published 1814, tell their story as the first U.S. citizens to travel from St. Louis to the Pacific.

The two cross through the magnificent Rocky Mountains, which hadn't been previously expored by U.S. explorers, and thus did not know how extensive they would be.

The Rocky Mountains stretch for thousands of miles acroos North America, and are only one part of Lewis and Clark's tale.

Eric Shipton: K2, Pakistan

Eric Shipton, often remembered as the man who was called off the first ascent of Everest, was more passionate about exploring mountains than just climbing the biggest ones.

Shipton wrote Blank on the Map, which showcases this spirit, describing an expedition to the Shaksgam area in the Karakoram mountains near K2.

The adventure and exploration was what drove Shipton, and thus key figures of the book are surveyors, who mapped the previously unexplored mountainous area.

Douglas Freshfield: Kangchenjunga, India/Nepal
